# Cold Email Writing Skill — GTMOS Reference

These principles are loaded automatically when writing or validating outbound copy.
Source: Cold Email Skill by Corey Haines.

---

## Core philosophy

Cold email is not marketing copy. It is a one-to-one message from a peer.
Write like a colleague sharing something useful — not a marketer broadcasting a pitch.

---

## Voice rules

1. **Peer voice** — Write as an equal. No "Dear Sir", no formal intros, no "I hope this finds you well". Talk like a smart colleague at a conference.
2. **Ruthless brevity** — Every word must earn its place. If you can say it in fewer words, do. First touch max 75 words. Follow-ups max 50 words.
3. **Observation-led** — Open with something you noticed about their company, role, or situation. Never open with yourself.
4. **No performance language** — No "excited to share", "thrilled to announce", "game-changing solution". These are spam signals.
5. **Specificity over cleverness** — A specific observation beats a clever hook every time. "I noticed you opened 3 new sales roles this month" > "Scaling your team?"

---

## Subject line rules

- 2-4 words, lowercase, no punctuation
- No clickbait, no questions, no emojis
- Must feel like an internal forward, not a newsletter
- Examples: `quick thought`, `re: hiring`, `your team`, `sales ops`
- Anti-examples: `Boost Your Revenue by 300%!`, `Quick Question for You`, `[First Name], check this out`

---

## Opening line rules

- Never start with "I" or your company name
- Never start with a compliment ("Love what you're doing at...")
- Open with an observation, a pattern, or a relevant fact
- The first line must create relevance in under 10 words
- Examples:
  - "Noticed {{company}} just opened 3 SDR roles."
  - "Most {{persona_title}}s at {{industry}} companies hit this wall around Series B."
  - "{{company}} is on 4 different tools for outbound — that usually means none of them work."

## Email frameworks

### Framework 1: Observation > Problem > Proof > Ask
Best for: first touch, cold open

1. **Observation** — Something specific about their company or role (1 sentence)
2. **Problem** — The pain that observation implies (1 sentence)
3. **Proof** — One concrete result or example (1 sentence)
4. **Ask** — Interest-based CTA (1 sentence)

### Framework 2: Pattern > Insight > Bridge > Ask
Best for: industry-level angle, thought leadership

1. **Pattern** — Something happening across their industry (1 sentence)
2. **Insight** — Why most people get it wrong (1 sentence)
3. **Bridge** — How this connects to what you do (1 sentence)
4. **Ask** — Interest-based CTA (1 sentence)

### Framework 3: Trigger > Relevance > Value > Ask
Best for: signal-triggered outreach, timely messages

1. **Trigger** — The event or signal you noticed (1 sentence)
2. **Relevance** — Why it matters to them specifically (1 sentence)
3. **Value** — What you can help with (1 sentence)
4. **Ask** — Interest-based CTA (1 sentence)

---

## CTA rules

- Always interest-based, never commitment-based
- Ask if they want to learn more, not if they want to buy
- One CTA per email — never stack asks
- Low friction: "Worth a look?" / "Make sense to chat?" / "Want me to send over the details?"
- Anti-examples: "Book a 30-minute demo", "Sign up for a free trial", "Let's schedule a call this week"

---

## Follow-up rules

Each follow-up must use a DIFFERENT angle. Never repeat the same pitch.

- **Touch 2:** New angle — different pain point, social proof, or industry insight
- **Touch 3:** Value add — share something useful (benchmark, insight, case study reference) without asking
- **Touch 4:** Breakup — honest, short, zero pressure. "Figured this isn't a priority right now — no worries. If it comes up later, happy to help."

Follow-up subject lines: reply to original thread (Re:) — never a new subject line.

---

## Quality checklist

Run this against every email before presenting:

| # | Check | Pass criteria |
|---|-------|--------------|
| 1 | Peer voice | Reads like a colleague, not a salesperson |
| 2 | Under word limit | Touch 1: 75 words. Touch 2+: 50 words |
| 3 | No "I" opener | First word is not "I" or company name |
| 4 | Observation-led | Opens with something specific about them |
| 5 | Single CTA | One ask, interest-based, low friction |
| 6 | No spam words | No "excited", "thrilled", "game-changing", "synergy", "leverage", "unlock" |
| 7 | Subject line | 2-4 words, lowercase, no punctuation |
| 8 | Angle rotation | Each touch uses a different angle than the previous |
| 9 | Specificity | At least one specific detail (company name, role, signal, metric) |
| 10 | Brevity | No filler sentences, no throat-clearing, no preamble |

---

## Audience calibration

Adjust tone based on persona seniority:

- **C-suite / VP:** Ultra-brief. Lead with business outcome. No feature talk. Max 50 words even on touch 1.
- **Director / Head of:** Slightly more context allowed. Can reference specific challenges at their level. Max 65 words.
- **Manager / IC:** Can include more specifics about workflow impact. Max 75 words.

---

## LinkedIn message adaptation

When writing LinkedIn messages (via Crispy), apply all the same principles with these adjustments:
- Even shorter — max 50 words for any LinkedIn message
- More conversational — LinkedIn is inherently less formal than email
- No attachments or links in first message
- Reference their profile, content, or activity when possible
- Connection request note: max 300 characters, no pitch, just relevance
