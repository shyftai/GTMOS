# Agency Objections Playbook

How to handle the six most common objections in agency new business. Load before reply handling when a prospect pushes back.

---

## Objection 1: "We do it in-house"

**How common:** Very common — especially from VP Marketing and Founder personas
**What it usually means:** Team has some marketing capability and believes they can handle things. OR they've had an agency fail them and decided to bring it in-house as a reaction.

**What to check before responding:**
- Are they actually doing it well? (Check their ad library, content output, LinkedIn activity)
- What's the gap? (Which channels are thin or missing?)
- Are they hiring? (Job postings suggest the in-house team is stretched)

**Acknowledge:**
Don't argue. Don't say "we're better than in-house." In-house teams have real advantages — relationships, context, speed. The reply should validate this.

**Reframe:**
"Most of our clients have in-house teams. We don't replace them — we extend them on the specific channels or campaigns where outside execution has a structural advantage."

**The positioning:**
- Agencies are better at: starting a new channel from zero, running high-volume outbound, providing specialised expertise (e.g. performance media buying at scale), covering capacity gaps during hiring or team transitions
- In-house is better at: brand voice, customer relationships, product knowledge, long-term strategy

**Proof angle:**
Share a one-liner: "[Company] had a 3-person marketing team. We added paid search alongside them. Combined result: pipeline up 3x in 90 days."

**Ask:**
"Which parts of your marketing are working well right now? And which parts are you least happy with?"

**The goal of this ask:** Surface the gap they admit to. That gap is the entry point.

## Objection 2: "We had a bad experience with an agency before"

**How common:** The most common and most valid objection in agency new business
**What it usually means:** They were burned. Slow delivery, no results, poor communication, over-promised and under-delivered. This is emotional, not just logical.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not dismiss the experience ("That's not how we work!")
- Do not immediately pitch yourself as different without acknowledging their pain
- Do not ask them what went wrong in the first touch — that's too much in cold outreach

**Acknowledge:**
Validate the experience. They're right to be cautious. Most agencies do disappoint.

**Reframe:**
"Most bad agency experiences come down to three things: misaligned expectations at the start, no transparency in reporting, and slow communication. We built our operating model around those three failures specifically."

**Specific accountability mechanisms to reference:**
- Named account lead (not a rotating account team)
- Weekly async update (not waiting for a monthly call)
- Clear 30/60/90 day milestones agreed upfront
- Kill clause in the contract (they can leave with 30 days notice if results don't show)

**Proof angle:**
"We were hired by [company] after they'd had a bad run with two agencies. We set clear milestones in the first week. Six months later, [result]."

**Ask:**
"What specifically went wrong? I want to make sure we're not structured the same way."

**Why this ask works:** It forces a specific answer, not a vague complaint. It shows confidence. It surfaces the actual failure mode so you can directly address it.

## Objection 3: "You're too expensive"

**How common:** Common — especially from Founder persona
**What it usually means:** They don't see the ROI clearly, OR they've anchored on a lower price from a competitor, OR they genuinely have budget constraints.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not drop price immediately — it signals that the original price was wrong
- Do not say "we're the best in the market" without proof
- Do not compare yourself to cheaper agencies in the first response

**Reframe — ROI first:**
"Expensive relative to what? A $10K/month agency that drives $120K in pipeline is cheaper than a $5K one that drives $20K."

**Ask before you justify:**
"What's your current cost of acquiring a customer? And what's a qualified meeting worth to you?"

Get them to anchor on the outcome, not the price. Then connect your pricing to the outcome.

**For Founder persona:**
"I hear you on price. What would make you feel confident the investment is worth it? We can structure a 60-day pilot with clear milestones — if we don't hit them, we'd rather you know that early."

**For CMO persona:**
"The question isn't what it costs — it's what it's worth if it works. We've helped three companies in your space improve CAC by 30–45% in the first quarter. At your MQL volume, what does a 30% CAC improvement mean in annual savings?"

**If genuine budget constraint:**
Offer a smaller scope at entry price, not a discount on full scope. "We can start with [one channel] at [starter price] — prove it works, then expand."

**Never:**
Drop price more than 10% without going to account director. Document all discounts in COSTS.md.

## Objection 4: "We're happy with our current agency"

**How common:** Common — especially from CMO persona
**What it usually means:** They have an existing agency and are not actively shopping. But "happy" can have degrees. There's almost always something that's not being done or not being done well.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not try to displace the existing agency in the first reply
- Do not ask "what are they not doing well?" immediately — too aggressive
- Do not attack the competitor agency

**Acknowledge:**
"That's great. You should be." (Short, genuine, not sycophantic.)

**Reframe — complementary positioning:**
"Most of our clients work with us alongside their existing agency. Usually on a specific channel or campaign the existing agency isn't focused on, or a one-time project with a clear deliverable."

**The opening:**
Start small. Offer a wedge project — not a full retainer replacement.

**Ask:**
"Is there anything you wish they did differently? Any channel that feels underserved or a campaign you haven't had capacity to run?"

**Why this works:** Almost every CMO has something they wish their agency did better. The ask doesn't threaten the existing relationship — it finds the gap.

**Follow-up if no gap found:**
"Understood. I'll stay in touch for when the timing is different." Log the prospect for nurture, set a 90-day reminder.

## Objection 5: "Not the right time / budget is frozen"

**How common:** Very common, especially in Q4 or post-fundraising lull
**What it usually means:** Could be real (budget freeze, org change, distraction) or could be a soft no. Hard to tell without asking.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not push for an immediate meeting
- Do not argue with the timing
- Do not disappear — this prospect is warm, just not ready

**Acknowledge:**
"Completely understand. Timing matters."

**Ask — to separate real from soft:**
"When does your budget cycle reset? I'll reach out then with something relevant." OR "What would need to change for the timing to be right?"

**If real timing issue:** Add to nurture, set a reminder for the right window (budget reset, next quarter, event trigger).

**If soft no:** Accept it gracefully. Leave the door open. "No problem. I'll check back in [X months] — if things change before then, feel free to reach out."

**Nurture touch (60 days later):**
Short, no pressure. "Checking in — budget cycle should be resetting about now. Anything has changed on your end? Happy to share what we've been seeing from others in [their industry]."

## Objection 6: "We need to think about it / discuss internally"

**How common:** Very common — almost always comes after a proposal or call
**What it usually means:** Normal buying process. They like it enough not to say no, but need internal alignment.

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not send a follow-up the next day asking if they've decided
- Do not send a "just checking in" with no new value
- Do not assume this is a soft no

**Acknowledge:**
"Of course — that's the right move for a decision this size."

**Help them sell internally:**
The most useful thing you can do is make it easy for them to champion the decision internally. Offer a one-page summary they can forward:

"Happy to put together a one-pager — the situation we'd be solving, our approach, what results to expect, and the investment. Makes it easier to share with whoever else needs to weigh in. Want me to send that over?"

**What the one-pager includes:**
1. The situation as you understand it (3 bullets)
2. What you'd do (3-4 bullets)
3. One comparable result
4. The investment and scope
5. The next step if they want to proceed

**Ask:**
"Who else should be on the next call?" — surfaces the decision maker if they're not already in the conversation.

**Follow-up timing:**
5–7 days after the conversation. Not the next day.

---

## Objection handling principles

1. **Acknowledge before you reframe.** Never argue with the objection. Start with "that makes sense" or a genuine acknowledgement before pivoting.
2. **Ask before you justify.** A question surfaces the real objection. A justification often misses it.
3. **Specifics beat generics.** "Here's what happened for [company]" beats "we're the best at [thing]" every time.
4. **Leave the door open.** Even when it's a no, don't burn the bridge. A graceful exit often re-opens later.
5. **Know when to stop.** Two objection exchanges is usually the limit on cold outreach. After two, move to nurture and set a long-term follow-up.
