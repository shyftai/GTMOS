# Pitch Frameworks

Agency pitch and proposal frameworks. Load before running `/agency:pitch`. All frameworks are evidence-first — never pitch a method without a result.

---

## Cold outreach openers (first touch frameworks)

### Framework 1: The Audit Open

**Best for:** Prospects where you've done real research on their marketing output.
**Why it works:** Specific > generic. An observation about their actual situation is more credible than any claim about your agency.

**Template:**
"Ran a quick [audit type] on [company]'s [channel]. Found [specific observation]. Happy to share the full analysis — usually takes 15 minutes."

**Audit types you can actually run:**
- Ad library check (Meta / Google — free, takes 3 minutes)
- Landing page / funnel review
- SEO snapshot (Semrush free tier)
- LinkedIn content audit (manually review their last 10 posts)
- Email subscribe and review their sequence

**Hard rule:** Only use this if you actually checked. Do not fabricate audit findings. One vague "audit open" destroys trust; one specific one builds it.

**Example (real):**
"Checked [company]'s Meta ad library — you're running the same 3 creatives you launched 4 months ago. Usually when we see that, ROAS is starting to compress. Took 15 minutes to confirm your suspicion. Want to see what we found and what we'd test first?"

---

### Framework 2: The Case Study Match

**Best for:** When you have a directly comparable case study — same vertical, similar company size, or matching problem.
**Why it works:** Proof from a peer company is the most persuasive thing you can put in a first touch.

**Template:**
"We worked with [similar company / industry] on [problem]. They went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]. Same situation seems to apply to [company]."

**What makes the case study credible:**
- Specific numbers (not "significant improvement")
- Specific timeframe (not "quickly")
- Anonymised correctly — use "[Series A HR tech company]" if you can't name them, but the industry and size must be real

**Example:**
"We worked with a B2B SaaS company at your stage that was running paid search with no attribution. Built the attribution model and found 40% of spend was going to keywords that hadn't converted in 6 months. Redirected it. CPL dropped 38% in 60 days. Happy to share how we approached it if you're running into anything similar."

---

### Framework 3: The Signal Reference

**Best for:** Signal-triggered outreach — funding, new hire, product launch.
**Why it works:** Timely relevance. Reaching someone right after a trigger event, with an interpretation of what that event means, feels like insight — not a pitch.

**Template:**
"Saw [company] just [trigger]. Usually that means [implication for marketing]. Happy to share what's worked for other companies at that stage."

**Timing rule:** The faster after the signal, the better. After 2 weeks, the moment has passed for funding and hire signals.

**Examples:**

Funding:
"Saw [company] just closed their Series B. Usually that means the board's growth expectations just jumped significantly. Happy to share what worked for three other SaaS companies right after their raise — CAC, pipeline coverage, and go-to-market motion."

New CMO:
"Saw you just joined [company] as CMO. New leaders usually do a full audit in the first 60 days — agencies included. Happy to share how we've worked with other new marketing leaders in that window. Even if the timing isn't right, it might be useful."

Product launch:
"Saw [company] just launched [product]. The launch moment is usually when distribution becomes the constraint. We've run 8 product launches in the last 18 months — happy to share what's worked and what's burned budget."

---

### Framework 4: The Contrarian Observation

**Best for:** When you have a genuine insight or counter-intuitive observation about their approach or their market.
**Why it works:** Stands out from the "let me know if you need marketing help" noise. Requires real knowledge.

**Template:**
"Most [company type] companies at your stage focus on [common approach]. The ones that grow fastest usually flip that — they [different approach]. Worth a conversation if that's interesting."

**Example:**
"Most Series A SaaS companies focus on top-of-funnel paid ads first. The ones that hit 3x growth in the first year usually start with outbound to warm ICP before turning paid on — it defines what converts before you pay to scale it. Happy to walk through the logic if it's relevant."

**Warning:** Only use a contrarian angle if it's genuine and defensible. A fake contrarian observation reads as consultancy theatre.

## Proposal frameworks

### 1-page brief (most common)

Use for: exploratory pitches, small scope, Founder persona, cases where you haven't had a discovery call yet.

**Structure:**

```
[Company Name] — Marketing Partnership

The situation:
[2–3 bullets describing what you understand about their challenge]

Our approach:
[3–4 bullets — what you'd do specifically for them]
- [Action 1 — specific, not generic]
- [Action 2]
- [Action 3]

Proof:
[One comparable result — specific numbers only]

The scope:
[Service line(s), retainer or project, monthly price range]

The next step:
[Sign → kick-off call in X days]
Or: [30-min call to confirm fit → proposal within 48 hours]
```

**Length:** Maximum one page / one screen. If it requires more, it's a full proposal.

---

### Full proposal (5–8 slides)

Use for: deals over $8K/month, CMO or VP Marketing buyer, when you've had at least one discovery call.

**Slide structure:**

**Slide 1: Your situation**
Show you understand their business, their goals, and the specific challenge. Use their language. This slide should feel like you've been listening — not like a template.

3–5 bullets. No agency branding. All about them.

**Slide 2: What we found**
Audit results or research findings. The observation that created urgency. Makes the rest of the proposal feel earned.

If you haven't done an audit, this slide becomes: "What companies at your stage typically face" — useful, but less powerful.

**Slide 3: Our approach**
Strategy and execution plan — specific to them, not a generic service description.

4–6 bullets. Include: what you'd do in days 1–30, what you'd measure, how you'd report.

**Slide 4: Case studies (2–3 examples)**
One case study per relevant service line or challenge. Match to their vertical or company type.
Include: company type (anonymised), challenge, approach, result.

**Slide 5: The team**
Who works on their account. Named people with roles.
Not "our team of experts." Names, titles, relevant experience.

**Slide 6: Pricing (3 tiers)**

| Tier | Services | Monthly |
|------|----------|---------|
| Starter | [scope] | $X |
| Growth | [scope] | $X |
| Scale | [scope] | $X |

Frame it: "Most clients start with Growth. Starter if you want to test one channel first."

**Slide 7: Next steps**
Three things:
1. What happens when they say yes (kick-off within X days)
2. What the first 30 days looks like
3. How to proceed (sign, call, or response)

## Proposal rules

- Every proposal must include at least one case study from CASE-STUDIES.md
- Every proposal must reference only services from SERVICE-LINES.md
- Every proposal must include pricing from PRICING.md (or explain deviation)
- Before sending, run the service line gate: does this promise anything we can't deliver?
- After sending: follow up in 7 days if no response
- If no response after two follow-ups: move to "negotiation stalled" in PIPELINE.md

## Internal champion doc

When a prospect says "we need to discuss internally" — offer this.

A 1-page document they can forward to the decision maker or finance:

```
[Agency Name] — Brief for [Company]

What we'd solve:
[1–2 sentences on the specific challenge]

How we'd approach it:
[3 bullets — specific, not generic]

What this looks like in practice:
[2 bullets — timeline and deliverables]

Proof it works:
[One-liner result from comparable company]

Investment:
[Price range or specific tier]

The risk:
[What doesn't happen without solving this — make it tangible]

If you want to move forward:
[Clear next step — email reply, call, or link]
```

## Pricing conversation frameworks

**When prospect raises price objection in the proposal:**

1. "Expensive relative to what?" — anchor on the outcome, not the price
2. Calculate ROI in their terms: "If [service] moves your CAC from $X to $Y, what does that mean annually?"
3. Offer starter tier, not a discount: "We can start with [narrower scope] to prove it — then expand"
4. Offer a pilot: "60-day sprint with clear milestones — if we don't hit them, you've learned something valuable with limited exposure"

**Never drop price on the first ask.** ROI reframe first, scope reduction second, discount only if the account director approves.
