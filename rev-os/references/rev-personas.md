# REV:OS — Buyer Personas

The people REV:OS serves — both as the primary operators and as the stakeholders who consume RevOps output.

---

## Persona 1: The RevOps Lead

**Titles:** Head of Revenue Operations, Director of RevOps, VP Revenue Operations, Revenue Operations Manager

**Company profile:** Series B–D B2B SaaS or high-growth tech; 50–500 employees; $5M–$100M ARR; using Salesforce or HubSpot as primary CRM; Stripe or similar for billing

**Day-to-day reality:**
- Owns the CRM and the data — and gets blamed when it's wrong
- Spends 40–60% of time firefighting data quality issues rather than doing analysis
- Runs or coordinates the weekly pipeline review and monthly forecast
- Handles ad-hoc data requests from sales, marketing, finance, and exec simultaneously
- Manages a stack of 8–15 tools, some integrated, some not
- Is frequently asked "why is the data different in X vs. Y?"

**What they care about:**
- Data they can trust — so they stop getting questions about mismatches
- Pipeline visibility that's actually predictive — not just a snapshot
- Forecast accuracy — being within 5–10% of actual consistently
- Time savings — fewer manual pulls, fewer firefighting sessions
- Alignment — getting sales, marketing, and CS to agree on definitions

**How they talk:**
- Direct, operational, metrics-fluent
- Comfortable with SQL, spreadsheets, and BI tools
- Skeptical of vendor claims; trust data over demos
- "What's the data source?" and "how was this calculated?" before trusting anything

**Buying signals:**
- New CRM implementation or migration
- Recent leadership change (new CRO, VP Sales, or CFO asking "why is the data wrong?")
- Post-funding round — new headcount means messy data
- Quarterly planning season — they're building the model and the data is garbage
- Announced tech stack change (leaving Salesforce for HubSpot or vice versa)

**Objections:**
- "We already have a BI tool" — they want analysis, not another dashboard layer
- "Our data is a mess, this won't help" — actually the opposite: this is exactly when REV:OS adds most value
- "I don't have bandwidth to onboard another tool" — REV:OS works with existing stack, no new tool install
- "Legal won't approve enrichment vendors" — work with what's already approved; flag gaps

**Copy guidance:**
- Lead with outcomes: "forecast within 5% every quarter" not "AI-powered revenue intelligence"
- Acknowledge the mess: "we know your CRM has 30% bad data — here's how we fix it systematically"
- Short sentences, specific numbers, no jargon
- Case studies over feature lists

## Persona 2: The RevOps Analyst

**Titles:** Revenue Operations Analyst, Sales Operations Analyst, GTM Analyst, Business Operations Analyst

**Company profile:** Series A–C; 30–200 employees; reports to Head of RevOps or VP Sales Ops; heavy spreadsheet user; often the person who builds all the reports

**Day-to-day reality:**
- Builds and maintains every report the sales team uses
- Gets pinged by AEs asking for data extracts constantly
- Manually reconciles CRM vs. Stripe vs. finance spreadsheet every month-end
- Knows where the bodies are buried — every data quirk, every edge case
- Runs the weekly pipeline calls with SDRs and AEs
- Lives in CRM reports, spreadsheets, and the data warehouse

**What they care about:**
- Fewer manual pulls and reconciliations
- Reports that update automatically
- A single source of truth everyone agrees on
- Getting credit for analysis, not just data entry
- Learning skills (SQL, BI, data modeling) that advance their career

**How they talk:**
- Detail-oriented, methodical
- "Actually, that number includes X which we normally exclude for this metric"
- Fluent in Excel/Sheets formulas and basic SQL
- Will immediately ask how something was calculated

**Buying signals:**
- LinkedIn job post for "RevOps Analyst" (team is growing, needs more capacity)
- Their company just raised and is scaling ops
- They're posting questions in RevOps communities about tooling or data problems

**Copy guidance:**
- Speak to the daily frustration: "stop spending 4 hours every month reconciling Stripe vs. Salesforce"
- Show the before/after: manual → automated
- Make them the hero — this makes their work better, not redundant

## Persona 3: The CRO / VP Sales

**Titles:** Chief Revenue Officer, VP Sales, VP Revenue, SVP Sales

**Company profile:** Series B–D; 50–500 employees; managing 5–50 AEs; owns the revenue number

**Day-to-day reality:**
- Owns the revenue number and lives or dies by it
- Runs weekly pipeline reviews — needs accurate data, not opinions
- Is frustrated when forecast misses because "the data was wrong"
- Relies on RevOps to make sense of the data; doesn't have time to dig themselves
- Makes resourcing and territory decisions based on what RevOps tells them

**What they care about:**
- Forecast accuracy — reliably knowing what will close this quarter
- Pipeline health — enough coverage to hit the number
- Rep performance — who's behind, who's at risk, who's coachable
- Deal inspection — what's actually happening in their top deals
- Competitive intel — what are they losing to and why

**How they talk:**
- Outcome-focused, time-compressed
- "Show me the number, then tell me why"
- Wants a single slide, not a spreadsheet
- Low tolerance for "it depends"

**Buying signals:**
- Missed a quarter — CRO is now demanding better forecast data
- Board asked about forecast accuracy
- New CRO hire — they want to rebuild the RevOps function their way
- Sales team is growing and pipeline visibility is breaking down

**Copy guidance:**
- Lead with the miss they're trying to avoid: "know your number before the last week of the quarter"
- Keep it strategic — they don't care about dedupe; they care about revenue predictability
- One number, one chart, one action per communication

## Persona 4: The CFO / VP Finance

**Titles:** CFO, VP Finance, Controller, Head of FP&A

**Company profile:** Series B+ with a finance team; $10M+ ARR; using Stripe or similar; probably building a board model

**Day-to-day reality:**
- Owns the financial model and the board pack
- Spends significant time reconciling CRM revenue with accounting records
- Gets surprised by churn that wasn't in the forecast
- Wants to trust the ARR number but often can't — too many definitions, too many systems
- Is the one who eventually escalates "why does our CRM show a different ARR than Stripe?"

**What they care about:**
- A single, auditable ARR/MRR definition everyone uses
- Stripe ↔ CRM reconciliation that doesn't require manual intervention
- Churn and expansion visibility before it hits the books
- Board-ready metrics that are defensible

**How they talk:**
- Precise, audit-minded
- "What's the definition?" and "how does this reconcile with the GL?"
- Will scrutinize methodology before trusting any number
- Wants a paper trail

**Copy guidance:**
- Lead with reconciliation and trust: "a single ARR number that finance and sales both trust"
- Audit trail matters: every number should be traceable
- Avoid fluffy language — use accounting-adjacent terms where appropriate
